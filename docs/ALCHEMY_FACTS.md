# 100 Fun Facts: The Chemistry of Alchemy

Drawn from our scholarship — Anna Marie Roos, *The Salt of the Earth* (Brill); Lawrence
Principe, *The Secrets of Alchemy*; William Newman, *Atoms and Alchemy* / *Gehennical
Fire* / *Promethean Ambitions* and Newman & Principe, *Alchemy Tried in the Fire*; Bruce
Moran, *Distilling Knowledge* / *The Alchemical World of the German Court*; material-
culture archaeology (Martinón-Torres & Rehren); Pamela Smith, *The Body of the Artisan*;
and the AlchemyTimelineMap concept base. Each fact carries a short source tag. These
facts drove the equipment redesign (see the mapping table at the end and HISTORIOGRAPHY.md).

## I. The tria prima and the salt principle

1. Paracelsus took the old mercury–sulphur pair and **added salt** to make the *tria
   prima*: salt was the principle that gave matter "solidity and bodily shape" — the
   body, to mercury's spirit and sulphur's soul. (Roos, ch.2)
2. Chymists held that every salt secretly held all three principles at once: a fixed
   earthy salt (plain salty taste), a nitrous/sulphureous salt (sweet, oily), and a
   mercurial sal ammoniac (sour). (Roos, ch.2)
3. Because salt was thought to carry taste and colour, **water — being saltless — was
   defined as "utterly unsavoury,"** i.e. literally tasteless. (Roos, ch.2)
4. Salt was the "balsam of nature": deer sought salt licks, and the Latin *salax*
   ("salty") gave us "salacious" — salt was linked to lust and fertility. (Roos, ch.2)
5. Duchesne threw out air and fire as elements (fire isn't in Genesis; air is just
   rarefied water), leaving a five-principle scheme: earth, water, salt, sulphur,
   mercury. (Roos, ch.2)
6. The three principles were "read off" a distillation: rising fumes were **mercury**,
   the flame was **sulphur**, and the leftover ash was **salt**. (Roos, ch.2)

## II. Fire, ash, and the ghosts of plants

7. In **palingenesis**, chymists burned a plant to ash, drew out its volatile salt, and
   warmed it gently — and swore ghostly salt-crystals shaped like stems and leaves rose
   up, "resurrecting" the plant. (Roos, ch.2–3)
8. Distilling three pints of vinegar gave first a tasteless watery phlegm, then a sharp
   biting liquor they thought was sal ammoniac — it was really **acetic acid**, not yet
   recognized. (Roos, ch.2)
9. Distilling vinegar over salt of tartar left a product that **weighed more than the
   tartar alone** — one of the earliest deliberate uses of the balance to track a salt
   by weight gain. (Roos, ch.2)
10. **Calcination** — heating a substance to ash — was reckoned the first of the great
    operations, opening a metal or mineral so the fire could work on it. (AlchemyTimelineMap)
11. **Sublimation** turns a solid straight to vapour and back to a solid crust, skipping
    the liquid — the aludel was the vessel built for it. (AlchemyTimelineMap)
12. The **quintessence** was the "fifth essence" beyond earth-water-air-fire, chased by
    distilling a spirit over and over until nothing grosser remained. (AlchemyTimelineMap;
    Rupescissa, 14th c.)

## III. Nitre and saltpetre — the "hermaphroditical" salt

13. Before industry, saltpetre was scraped off cave walls and privies, or **farmed in
    "nitre-beds"** — piles of manure, ash, and straw kept wet with urine, then leached
    and fixed into potassium nitrate. (Roos, ch.2)
14. Nitre was called "male and female, fixed and volatile, fire and ice": it flares hot
    as gunpowder, yet **dissolving it in water chills the solution** (a real endothermic
    reaction). (Roos, ch.2)
15. Heating saltpetre in a glass tinted it with colours "of no less variety than flowers
    in the spring" — taken as proof that colour lived *inside* the salt. (Roos, ch.2)
16. Sylvius thought the very air carried **nitric acid on the north wind and volatile
    alkali on the south**, and that their meeting made cold, "as when sal ammoniac
    dissolves in water." (Roos, ch.4)

## IV. Glauber, vitriol, and the salts that "swell with the tides"

17. Johann Glauber found his "**sal mirabile**" (Glauber's salt) after a spring water
    cured his stomach — it forms when oil of vitriol acts on common salt:
    2 NaCl + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2 HCl. (Roos, ch.2)
18. Glauber guessed nitre was the **alkahest** (universal solvent) because it could be
    driven to opposite extremes — into corrosive nitric acid, or into caustic potash. (Roos, ch.2)
19. He **disproved his own claim** by recombining the acid and the potash and getting
    plain nitre back — a result Boyle also published in his 1661 *Essay on Nitre*. (Roos, ch.2)
20. Glauber used nitric acid as a **colourist's kit**: copper or lead in it gave green,
    it whitened wax, stained quills gold, and glazed pottery purple. (Roos, ch.2)
21. The old **VITRIOL acrostic** hid the Stone: *Visita Interiora Terrae Rectificando
    Invenies Occultum Lapidem* — "visit the interior of the earth; by rectifying you will
    find the hidden stone." (Roos, ch.2)
22. Oil of vitriol (sulphuric acid) is so thirsty for water that Zwinger watched a dish of
    it **visibly swell and shrink with the tides** — really just soaking up humidity. (Roos, ch.3)
23. **Adding water to oil of vitriol** produces a "dangerous explosive reaction" — violent
    exothermic heat, a hazard Boyle specifically warned about. (Roos, ch.3–4)
24. Sal ammoniac is likewise hygroscopic: Boyle measured a sal-ammoniac solution **gaining
    "near a dram" in weight from the air alone.** (Roos, ch.3)

## V. Living metals, the wolf, and the green lion

25. Vitriol (iron sulphate) blooms as a green tarnish when pyrites weather in moist air, so
    miners read the acidic "gur" seeping from ore as a **"metallical seed."** (Roos, ch.2–3)
26. Miners believed metals **grew and ripened underground like fruit**: arsenic and bismuth
    were "immature," and gold was the fully ripe end-state. (Roos, ch.2)
27. Pull a metal too early and you doomed it — its "life, consisting in a volatile salt,"
    would be drawn back to the stars and it would be "eaten up." (Roos, ch.2)
28. **Antimony was the "wolf of metals"**: added to a molten mix it "eats" the base metals
    and clings to the gold, parting the precious from the impure. (Roos, ch.2)
29. "**Glass of antimony**" — antimony fused with sulphur — could take any colour of the
    spectrum, argued as proof it held all qualities, like a true salt. (Roos, ch.2)
30. Starkey (as "Eirenaeus Philalethes") prepared **sophic mercury** using the *star
    regulus of antimony* — a purified metal that crystallizes in a radiating star — the
    key operation Newman & Principe reconstructed in the lab. (Newman, *Gehennical Fire*;
    Newman & Principe)

## VI. The mineral acids and their bite

31. **Aqua regia** (nitric + hydrochloric) dissolves *both* gold and silver — a "unity of
    opposites," since alchemists gendered gold male and silver female. (Roos, ch.2)
32. **Aqua fortis** (nitric acid) eats silver but spares gold — which is exactly why it is
    the assayer's tool for **parting** the two metals. (Roos; assay practice)
33. Newton bluntly **equated the alchemists' "sulphur" with acid**: "by sulphur they mean
    acid," and said trapped acid is what makes oily bodies inflammable. (Roos, ch.4;
    Newton, *De Natura Acidorum*, 1710)
34. Newton held acid particles were mid-sized, with "a great attractive force," rushing at
    matter to "excite heat" and shake it apart — "in all Fermentation there is an Acid
    latent." (Roos, ch.4)
35. Newton cited **spirit of vitriol on iron filings** dissolving them "with a great heat
    and ebullition" as visible proof of short-range attractive force. (Roos, ch.4)

## VII. Acid, alkali, and effervescence

36. Sylvius built a whole physiology on acid–alkali reactions: bile alkaline, pancreatic
    juice acidic, and **their mixing effervesces and gives off heat** — the model reaction
    of early chemistry. (Roos, ch.4)
37. Boyle sorted salts into three classes — **acid, fixed alkali (lixivial), and volatile
    (urinous)** — a classification still legible in modern acid/base chemistry. (Roos, ch.4)
38. Boyle built early **pH indicators** from syrup of violets: acids turn it red, alkalis
    turn it green. (Roos, ch.3–4)
39. "**Fixed**" salts resist the fire and stay put (potash); "**volatile**" salts fume and
    decompose on gentle heat (sal ammoniac, ammonium carbonate — smelling salts). (Roos, ch.3)
40. Grew turned flowers into a chemistry set: **spirit of sulphur reddened violet
    tincture; spirit of hartshorn (ammonia) turned borage "verdigrease green."** (Roos, ch.3)
41. Grew's colour rule read salt ratios straight off: green = alkali + sulphur; yellow =
    balanced; blue = sulphur-dominant; scarlet = strongly sulphur-dominant. (Roos, ch.3)

## VIII. Crystals, weather, and the shape of salts

42. Grew called salts "**the bones of the plants**" and matched crystal shapes to matter:
    marine salt made right angles, alkaline salts oblique, acid salts spirals. (Roos, ch.3)
43. Supersaturating water with several salts at once, Grew found water "full" of one salt
    could still take up another — proof, he said, that **each salt's atoms had a different
    figure**. (Roos, ch.3)
44. Grew noticed **dissolving salt made the water shrink**, and concluded there were
    "vacuities" in water where the salt lodged. (Roos, ch.3)
45. **Crystallization** — coaxing ordered solids out of solution — was how chymists
    isolated and recognized a purified salt. (AlchemyTimelineMap)
46. Lister classified spa-water salts largely by their **crystal shapes under the
    microscope**, following Leeuwenhoek. (Roos, ch.3)
47. Lister proved **vitriol forms only when pyrites meet air, not water** — pyrites kept
    underwater made no salt — refuting Van Helmont's "hungry hermaphroditical salt." (Roos, ch.3)
48. Lister blamed thunder and lightning on the "**inflammable breath of the pyrites**" —
    a theory Benjamin Franklin cited in the *Pennsylvania Gazette* in 1737. (Roos, ch.3)
49. Grew even read **snowflakes as nitre crystals**, matching their shoots to niter's
    cylindrical growths. (Roos, ch.3)

## IX. Salts, medicine, and quackery

50. Excess salt was thought to "**coagulate into tartar**," whose deposits blocked the
    body's channels — the classic case being gout, its concretions likened to
    chemically-grown crystals. (Roos, ch.2)
51. Mayerne dosed patients **sugar of lead** (lead acetate) to "sweeten" salty humours —
    sweet-tasting and, as we now know, poisonous. (Roos, ch.2)
52. Lockyer's famous "**Pills Extracted from Sunbeams**" were really antimony, nitre,
    salt, and charcoal fired in a crucible — marked up an estimated **64 times**. (Roos, ch.5)
53. **Smelling salts** (sal volatile — volatile ammonia salts) are the living survivor of
    the whole "vital salt" tradition. (Roos, ch.1, ch.4)
54. **Iatrochemistry** — Paracelsus's program of chemically-prepared medicines — put the
    laboratory at the centre of medicine. (AlchemyTimelineMap; Paracelsus)

## X. From "vital salt" to oxygen

55. Robinson argued acid in the air keeps animals alive, citing **spirit of nitre on oil
    of cloves bursting into flame even in a vacuum** — proof, he thought, that acid
    preserves fire and the heart. (Roos, ch.4)
56. Around 1752 Hales dropped **pyrites into aqua fortis** under a glass over water and got
    a brisk fizz, a black cloud (sulphur dioxide), and consumed air — his model for
    earthquakes. (Roos, ch.3)
57. Roos's throughline: the Paracelsian "**aerial vital salt**" became a universal saline
    acid, then an "acidifying principle," and finally — via Hales on respiration —
    **Lavoisier's oxygen**, literally the "acid-generator." (Roos, conclusion; Smith review)
58. So the modern word **oxygen** carries a fossil of alchemical salt-theory: it means
    "acid-former," from the belief that acidity was the key principle of matter. (Roos,
    conclusion)

## XI. Distillation, the signature operation

59. **Distillation** — driving off the volatile and catching it again — was chymistry's
    emblematic operation, and Moran makes it the craft matrix of the Scientific
    Revolution. (Moran, *Distilling Knowledge*)
60. The **alembic** (cap) sat atop the **cucurbit** (body); vapour rose, hit the cool cap,
    and ran down the beak into the receiver. (Moran; standard apparatus)
61. **Cohobation** meant pouring the distillate back onto its residue and distilling again
    and again — refining by patient repetition. (Moran; RESEARCH_TRACK "Cohobation")
62. The **retort** — a single-piece gourd with a bent neck — was the vessel for distilling
    the corrosive mineral acids like aqua fortis. (assay & acid practice)
63. The **bain-marie** (water bath) gave gentle, even heat and is named for **Maria the
    Jewess**, a founding figure of Greek alchemy credited with the tribikos still. (history
    of apparatus)
64. **Circulation** cycled a substance through the same operations — distil, dissolve,
    coagulate — over and over, to "perfect" it. (AlchemyTimelineMap)
65. **Fermentation** and **putrefaction** (the blackening, *nigredo*) were prized as
    signs of transformation, not spoilage — decay was the door to renewal. (AlchemyTimelineMap)

## XII. Fire, crucibles, and the assay

66. Medieval **Hessian crucibles**, fired past 1,300 °C, unknowingly synthesized **mullite**
    (Al₆Si₂O₁₃), giving them legendary resistance to thermal shock. (Martinón-Torres &
    Rehren)
67. By the 16th century **standardized crucibles were mass-produced**, which Martinón-Torres
    says helped fuel a "surge in experimentation with matter." (Smith review)
68. **Cupellation**, the fire assay: on a porous bone-ash **cupel**, a blast of air drives
    base metals into the ash as litharge (lead oxide) and leaves a bright bead of pure gold
    or silver. (assay archaeology)
69. **Parting** separated gold from silver with aqua fortis; **cementation** drew base
    metal out of gold leaf packed hot in salt and brick-dust. (assay practice)
70. The **balance** was the assay's judge: the *weight* of the final bead was the measured
    truth of the ore — chymistry made quantitative. (Newman & Principe)
71. Excavated glass and crucibles from **Tycho Brahe's Uraniborg laboratory** still carry
    mercury, arsenic, and lead — the workshop's air was genuinely poisonous. (Uraniborg
    excavation, 1988–90)
72. Tycho pursued not gold but **medicamenta tria**, three chemical medicines against
    plague — court alchemy as pharmacy. (AlchemyTimelineMap)

## XIII. Chrysopoeia — gold-making taken seriously

73. Newman & Principe revived the neutral term "**chymistry**" to name the undivided
    art, dissolving the false split between "good chemistry" and "bad alchemy." (Newman &
    Principe, 1998)
74. **Chrysopoeia** (gold-making) was a serious, reasoned experimental program, not mere
    fraud — the central claim of the new historiography. (Newman & Principe)
75. Principe **grew the "Philosophers' Tree"** (*arbor Dianae*) in the lab: from a mercury-
    and-silver amalgam a glittering silver tree branches in the flask — a real, repeatable
    demonstration the old texts described. (Principe, *The Secrets of Alchemy*)
76. Recipes hid behind **Decknamen** (cover-names) like "our Diana" and "the Doves of
    Diana"; decoding them is what let modern scholars run the operations again. (Principe)
77. Newman's *Promethean Ambitions* shows the deep debate over **art versus nature** —
    could human art truly perfect nature, or only imitate it? — that alchemy forced. (Newman)
78. Boyle, far from alchemy's enemy, **practised chrysopoeia and Helmontian chymistry**;
    he even lobbied to repeal the English statute against multiplying gold. (Newman &
    Principe)

## XIV. Reagents and operations, quick hits

79. **Cinnabar** (mercury sulphide, HgS) is a brilliant red ore; roast it and mercury
    vapour boils off, condensing to the liquid metal — a dramatic, toxic demonstration.
    (mineral chemistry; alchemical staple)
80. **Sal ammoniac** (ammonium chloride) sublimes cleanly and was a key flux and reagent
    carried into Europe from Arabic chymistry. (Roos; history of reagents)
81. **Salt of tartar** (potassium carbonate) is *deliquescent* — left out, it soaks water
    from the air into a syrup, "**per deliquium**." (Roos, ch.4)
82. **Aqua vitae** — distilled and rectified spirit of wine (ethanol) — was alchemy's great
    solvent and cordial, the "water of life." (Moran; iatrochemistry)
83. **Verdigris** (copper acetate) — the green formed by acetic acid on copper — was a
    prized pigment made in the lab. (Roos; colour chemistry)
84. **Litharge** (lead(II) oxide) is the yellow-red dross skimmed and absorbed during
    cupellation — the base metal made visible as it leaves the gold. (assay chemistry)
85. **Regulus** meant the purified metallic button that sank to the bottom of a crucible;
    the **star regulus of antimony** was especially prized. (Newman & Principe)
86. **Vitriol** came in colours named for planets: green (iron), blue (copper), white
    (zinc) — the same "vitriol" root behind sulphuric acid, "oil of vitriol." (Roos)
87. **Dissolution** and **coagulation** — "solve et coagula," dissolve and re-fix — were the
    paired rhythm at the heart of the work. (AlchemyTimelineMap)

## XV. The lab as a place of real knowledge

88. Alchemists kept **laboratory notebooks** with dated, quantitative trials; Starkey's are
    a founding document of experimental record-keeping. (Newman & Principe, *Alchemy Tried
    in the Fire*)
89. **Material culture** — the crucibles, glassware, and furnace-slag in the ground — now
    reads alchemical practice that the secretive texts left out. (material-culture approach)
90. **Artisanal epistemology** (Pamela Smith): the alchemist's knowledge lived in the hands
    and senses — colour, smell, the feel of a melt — not only in books. (Smith, *The Body of
    the Artisan*)
91. **Tacit knowledge** (Polanyi): much lab skill was unwritten and learned only by doing,
    which is why reconstruction (actually running the recipes) recovers so much. (Polanyi;
    Principe's method)
92. The **athanor** was a self-feeding "slow Harry" furnace that held a steady gentle heat
    for the days-long *digestions* the Great Work required. (history of apparatus)
93. **Luting** — sealing joints with clay paste — kept vapours and fire in; a failed lute
    meant a lost distillation or an explosion. (lab practice)
94. A **fume hood / flue** over the furnace drew off the toxic vapours; ventilation was a
    real and necessary part of the working lab. (Moran; Uraniborg evidence)
95. The metallurgical manuals — **Biringuccio's *Pirotechnia* (1540)** and **Agricola's *De
    re metallica* (1556)** — document furnaces, assays, and safety as everyday craft. (Moran)

## XVI. Decline, afterlife, and the human dimension

96. Alchemy wasn't abandoned as "disproven" — Fors shows it was **pushed aside as vulgar
    and "feminine,"** like magic and astrology, more a change of taste than of evidence.
    (Smith review)
97. Around 1800 "classical alchemy went extinct, but modern esoteric alchemy emerged and
    still thrives" — the split we live with today. (Priesner, in Smith review)
98. Both astrology and alchemy fed **identity and self-knowledge** — the alchemist's inner
    "transformation" a companion to the outer Work. (Smith review)
99. Standardized mass-produced apparatus meant the 16th–17th-century lab was a genuine
    **experimental-industrial workshop**, not a lone mystic's cell. (material culture)
100. The deepest fun fact: alchemists **failed to make gold but discovered real chemistry**
     — acids, salts, gases, quantitative method — the accidental treasure of a two-thousand-
     year search. (the new historiography, passim)
